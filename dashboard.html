01The Direct Answer

To Your Core Question

✓ YES, the field IS measurably changing

The field has weakened by approximately 10-15% since the 1800s, the North Magnetic Pole is accelerating rapidly toward Siberia at roughly 55 kilometers per year, and a region of weakened field called the South Atlantic Anomaly has grown significantly since 2014. These are real, documented changes that satellite missions like ESA's Swarm continue to monitor closely.

✗ NO, there is no credible evidence of imminent reversal

The scientific consensus is absolute on this point: geomagnetic reversals are fundamentally unpredictable and occur through a statistically random (Poisson) process with no detectable precursors. The last full reversal happened approximately 780,000 years ago—far longer than the average interval of ~450,000 years—but this statistical outlier does not indicate an imminent reversal. Current prediction models like WMM2025 and IGRF-14 can reliably forecast field conditions only about 5 years ahead; they cannot and do not attempt to predict reversals.

Field Strength Details

The overall decline of roughly 6.3% per century over the past 200 years represents a substantial shift in a planetary-scale phenomenon. Field strength at the surface now ranges from 25 to 65 microteslas depending on location.

Important context: Paleomagnetic evidence shows that current field strength is actually average for the past 7,000 years, and that the 10-15% decline since the 1800s falls within historical variation patterns.

South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA)

  • Location: Southeast South America, extending across South Atlantic to Africa
  • Cause: Reverse flux patches at core-mantle boundary where field lines loop back into Earth
  • Evolution: Grew by ~½ continental Europe since 2014
  • Recent acceleration: Weakening southwest of Africa accelerated since 2020
  • Radiation impact: Satellites passing through receive higher radiation doses

07Honest Assessment of Uncertainties

Is Current Behavior "Normal" or "Unusual"?

Arguments for "Normal": Current strength average for last 7,000 years; 10-15% decline within paleomagnetic variation; field has recovered from similar declines before. Arguments for "Unusual": SAA development unprecedented; 781,000-year Brunhes period unusually long. Status: Open scientific question.

When Will the Next Reversal Occur?

Pattern is statistically random (Poisson process). Average interval ~450,000 years. Last reversal 780,000 years ago. No "precursor" signals identified. Reversal could occur tomorrow or in 500,000 years. All claims of prediction are scientifically invalid.

How Fast Will Future Reversals Be?

Evidence: Laschamp: ~690 years; Brunhes-Matuyama: 200-22,000 years; Computer simulations can produce rapid reversals. Uncertainty: Duration varies by location; each reversal may differ; cannot predict duration of future events.

What Will Be the Effects?

Known: Increased radiation during weak field periods; technological disruption likely; climate effects possible but uncertain. Unknown: Severity of biological impacts; climate feedback mechanisms; human adaptation capacity.

"The Earth's magnetic field is genuinely weakening and changing, the North Pole is moving rapidly toward Siberia, and the South Atlantic Anomaly is growing—all of which are well-documented and actively monitored. However, these changes fall within the range of normal geological variation and do not provide evidence of an imminent reversal. Geomagnetic reversals are fundamentally unpredictable events that follow a random statistical process, and no scientific method exists to predict them in advance."
